EYEBROW: Field Note · Debrief
TITLE: Laurens, SC: TTG Regional Performance Debrief
BYLINE: By Brian R. Sells · May 2026 · Tactical Games · ~16 min read

[LEAD]
I just finished my second Tactical Games and I want to debrief my performance and thinking on how I'll adjust training for the next one. Across eight stages there was one consistent diagnosis, and writing it out is the most useful thing I can hand to anyone else trying to do this on a working person's schedule.

Quick context on me: former Ranger, current software exec, and someone who does **not** have the luxury of training all day. This was my second event. The first time out I competed in the Mens Tactical Division and landed around the 50th percentile. I performed ok physically, was not fully prepared for the level of weight and volume but still finished each event with the time cap. My rifle was my biggest mistake, I only had a 3x optic. I didn't realize tactical games favors precision shooting.

This time I trained more strength and conditioning and got a 1x8 LVPO Vortex Strike Eagle. I did drop down a divsion to Mens Intermediate. For my second time, I finished in the top 10% (6 in Divison and 34 Overall). Because I dropped to a more appropriate division, I'm not pretending that jump is apples-to-apples. But the lessons below still hold true and apply to any division.

> My fitness was never the problem. Precision shooting under fatigue was.

That's the whole post in one line. If you take nothing else: working professionals tend to over-train the engine and under-train the gun, because lifting and conditioning are easy to schedule and shooting under stress isn't. I lived that gap on every stage. This year with my busy schedule I just didn't have the time to really get comfortable with my new LVPO. Here's the walk-through.

## Stage 1 — "UnbeWeaveable"

Bike erg into rifle steel: 10-inch circles at 220 yards, downhill, shot off a tank trap. Eight rounds per iteration, the first 3x rounds mags were loaded, second 3x rounds you had to reload your mags while on the bike. Every miss bought you penalty work — weaving jerry cans over, under, and through an obstacle. Six rounds total, 15-minute cap.

I got through four rounds. The killer wasn't the bike or the carries — the bike was _easy_. The killer was that **I didn't have confidence in my holds.** It took me until the fourth round to figure out where in the reticle I actually needed to aim. By then I'd burned huge chunks of time on penalty weaves I never should have earned. On an unfamiliar tank-trap position I was muscling the rifle, the reticle was floating, and I was basically praying the shot would break clean.

[LESSONS]
- **Know your holds cold.** Be so comfortable with your optic and your holds that stress can't take them from you. This became the theme of the entire weekend.
- **Train different shooting positions positions.** Basics let me muscle through, but it cost real accuracy. I need a stable platform in awkward spots, not brute force.
- **Bring towels.** Dirty hands gunk up mags and actions. A stack of small towels saved me all weekend.

## Stage 2 — "AGC: Pistol Off-Hand"

Weather forced a modification, and also a stage to be cut. I didn't not shoot this event.

Honestly, I felt prepared here — it's the kind of thing we drill a lot. Not much to fix.

## Stage 3 — "Muddy Buddies"

A low-crawl gauntlet: run down to grab a sandbag, over and under the bars (two high, three low), back for rifle and pistol mags, then shoot both standing unsupported. Four rounds. Rifle was an 8-inch square at ~46 yards; pistol a 10-inch circle at ~26.

This was the first event of the weekend and it showed. My pistol was jerky — follow-through wasn't there, and I think I was slightly off zero and corrected on the fly. Still shot decent, but I gave up 10 to 15 hits I would've had if I hadn't been rusty/cold. Speed-wise my time was solid; **the penalties from missed shots are what hurt me.** Same story: the engine was fine, the marksmanship cost me. Good will only get you so far.

[LESSONS]
- **Towels, again.** Got filthy; my pistol optic gunked up from crawling through mud puddles. Started getting some jams (fortunately a tap was all that was needed to prevent needlessly shucking rounds). Keep the weapon clean and clear at all costs.
- **Pistol fundamentals are everything.** Follow-through and a confirmed zero. I got better as the day went on — which means it's just making fundamentals habit.
- **The secret sauce:** precision under fatigue. Practice stress _into_ rifle and pistol from a variety of positions. Body position and fundamentals matter more than anything.

## Stage 4 — "Run, It's the Cops"

A half-mile run in a plate carrier. Did well. Note to self: keep pushing VO2 max and running-specific conditioning. This is the easy stuff for me to schedule. Both TTGs have had some type of heavy cardio event like this. I offset strength and conditioning cycles which kept this good. My cardio days are the first to get cut so being more disciplined there (especially on travel when cardio is the easy button with hotel gyms).

## Stage 5 — "Hux Around and Find Out"

Connex towers. Up to the second floor, six pistol steel from 25–40 yards, then drop the mag and hit a seventh plate with your last round. Up to the third floor, transition to rifle, six targets from 120–300 yards off a tight balcony. 180-second cap.

I did well here. Pistol started shaky on the first two tagets but once I trusted my fundamentals I was drilling steel fast enough that the RO commented on it. Moving to the third floor, the rifle balcony was cramped. I was muscling into a supported position, again same recurring weakness of shooting from varied positions and the need to be more comfortable/confident there.

[LESSONS]
- **Position comfort under constraint.** I still can't build a stable rifle platform fast enough in tight or awkward spots.
- **Confidence in holds = speed.** The moment I trusted my data and fundamentals, my splits collapsed. Hesitation is a hold problem in disguise.

## Stage 6 — "Bolt Bounce"

Rucked was worn whole stage. Farmer carry downhill in 50-yard increments to 150 total, run back, then shoot: kneeling rifle from a barricade window and standing pistol. Rifle moved 600 → 400 → 200 yards by round; pistol steel at 47 yards. Every miss bought a hand-release burpee over a box — with the ruck on.

Fitness-wise I was rock solid here; plenty of people didn't finish. My misses came on rifle at 400 and 600 — distance, again, under fatigue. Overall I shot good, not great. 600/400 just needed a little more confidence in holds and different shooting positions (this one was from kneeling).

[LESSONS]
- **Proper holds and at distance.** Know exactly what to hold at 200, 400, 600 with my optic — and be comfortable reaching out there.
- **Holds are useless without a stable position.** The two problems are really one problem. Moving reticles aren't helpful at distance.

## Stage 7 — "Burn Pit Shuttle"

Pure fitness: a shuttle sprint moving four sandbags of varying weights across varied distances. I did well here — the engine showed up like it has all weekend — but I tripped once and lost time I won't get back. Annoying, not catastrophic.

What this stage actually tells me is that I need to keep building strength specifically for **awkward, off-center loads in a stress carry.** Sandbags don't behave like barbells. They sag, shift, and force you to brace asymmetrically — and TTG _will_ put them in front of you, every single time.

This will be critical for moving up divisions where the weights increase.

## Stage 8 — "Feds and Sleds"

Five rounds of sled drag with sandbags, roughly 175–200m per round, then shooting rifle and pistol from a barricade. Rifle was alternating sides of the barricade in kneeling/seated; pistol was through the center port, kneeling. Targets: 6-inch pistol steel at 20 yards, 6-inch rifle steel at 47.

This was my best stage of the weekend. I was pretty quick and last round of shooting was clean — every hit, in rhythm. One small decision made most of the difference: I used a _seated_ position for the left-side barricade shots and still fired from my right shoulder. TTG doesn't penalize how much body you expose, so I traded "tactical" for "stable" — and the stable position paid for itself in hits.

Honestly, the bigger pattern across the weekend was that I got more dialed in as it went on. By Stage 8 the fundamentals I'd been re-fining all weekend — hold confidence, follow-through, pulling the slack out of the trigger, holding the sight picture instead of getting excited — were just there. Not a breakthrough, just reps adding up.

[LESSONS]
- **Stability beats posture.** If the rules don't penalize body exposure, use the most stable position you can build. Hits are the point.
- **Warm-up matters.** I shoot better deep into a match than at the start. That's a training problem, not a competition problem — I need to show up dialed in, not get there mid-weekend. This was tied to a rough 2026 with a ton of work/personal travel and limited range time. I did mostly dry fire leading up to the comp.
- **Sled drags and ISO lunge holds.** Leg fatigue dropped competitors I watched all weekend. Keep that chain strong.

## The pattern — and the plan

Read those eight debriefs and the diagnosis is the same on every shooting stage: my conditioning carried me. My shooting under stress — specifically **known holds plus a stable position plus a calm trigger while fatigued** — is the entire opportunity. I got more dialed in as the weekend went on, which tells me the gap is trainable. The work now is to show up dialed in on Stage 1, not Stage 7 or 8.

Being on the road and working weekends during 2026 probably prevented me getting a podium this time. This is something I'll work to correct as I try to schedule training time to improve.

So here's the training progression I'm building around, and it doesn't require living at the range:

**1. Get comfortable finding positions.** Build a barricade and just rep getting _into_ stable positions confidently.
**2. Build accuracy at distance.** Dial holds in prone first, then move to alternate positions. Goal is two range days per month.
**3. Add the stress.** Practice getting into position under stress, then breaking the shot under stress.

That's the next chapter of this site: a barricade build in the garage, and an experiment I can't stop thinking about — a way to _simulate_ the distance dry-fire reps I need at home (more to come from my software/product brain). The targets at 400–600 are tiny in the reticle, so I'm toying with a laser target with an adjustable window that scales to the right apparent size + a sensor that registers a hit and reports it to my phone.

If I build it, you'll see it here first.

[CLOSING]
If you're a busy professional chasing the same thing, the takeaway is simple: audit where your training hours go. Mine were going to the part that was already strong. Yours might be too.
